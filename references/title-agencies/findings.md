# Title Agency Reference Pulse-Check

Three sites chosen for the spread they show: the SaaS-side ceiling (Qualia), the legacy-giant baseline (First American), and the cautionary tale of a disruptor that didn't quite land (Doma). Stewart and Endpoint were considered — both reinforce the "tired corporate" pattern without adding new signal. (Endpoint just got absorbed into First American — its site is now a transitional placeholder.)

---

## 1. Qualia — https://www.qualia.com

**Positioning:** Closing software platform for title agents, lenders, and homebuyers. The category-defining B2B SaaS player.

**Visual treatment:** Deep navy + bright teal accent, generous white, mid-density product-led layout. Sans-serif system stack (Inter-adjacent), bold 48–64px headlines paired with 16–18px body. Hero leads with a product announcement ("Clear 2.0"), not a homeowner photo. Customer logos (Fidelity, First American, Redfin) sit above the fold as social proof. Real product UI screenshots replace stock photography. Subtle vector illustrations only in the footer.

**What works:**
- Trust earned through specificity — SOC 2 and ALTA-ALIGN badges occupy the slot where a stock "secure" icon would normally go.
- Multi-stakeholder navigation splits cleanly (title cos / agents / homebuyers).
- Product UI screenshots do the heavy lifting; no decorative imagery filler.

**What's tired:**
- Standard SaaS chassis — pricing link, demo CTA, webinar resource grid. Could be Gusto or Rippling.
- Teal-on-navy is the modern fintech default; safe, not distinctive.

**Adopt:** **Partial.** Steal the discipline — real proof > decorative icons, customer logos above fold, screenshots > stock photos. Skip the navy/teal palette and SaaS chassis. Cloud Title is a service brand, not a platform.

---

## 2. First American Title — https://www.firstam.com

**Positioning:** 130-year-old title insurance giant. The category default.

**Visual treatment:** Neutral foundation, corporate blue accent, vertically-stacked modular sections. No single hero — a segmented card layout with "We don't just close deals — we open possibilities" as the headline. Mix of icons, award badges, and economic-research photography. Dense navigation; multiple audience pathways collapsed onto the homepage.

**What works:**
- Trust signals are concrete (awards, economic center, fraud-prevention CTA) rather than vague "trusted since" copy.
- Modular section-cards survive at any breakpoint — natively Divi-friendly.

**What's tired:**
- Headline is a paraphrase of every corporate real-estate site since 2012.
- Default sans-serif, generic blue, no typographic point of view.
- Dense secondary nav reads "we serve every market" — flattens the message.

**Adopt:** **No on aesthetic, yes on architecture.** The modular section-card pattern is exactly what Divi does well — use it. Reject palette, voice, and the audience-grid approach.

---

## 3. Doma — https://www.doma.com

**Positioning:** "The future of title insurance" — AI-first disruptor. (Post-IPO struggles; the site reads as residual marketing.)

**Visual treatment:** Custom theme, clean hierarchy, mission-first hero ("dramatically reduce the cost and complexity"). Streamlined nav, minimal imagery, B2B aesthetic prioritizing function over flair.

**What works:**
- Direct, purpose-driven headline beats vague "trusted partner" language.
- Restraint — no stock handshake photos, no gradient blobs.

**What's tired:**
- Reads as a 2021 fintech template, frozen in time.
- "The future of X" is the most overused B2B hero formula in the category.
- No visual personality beyond the logo — could be any pre-Series-B startup.

**Adopt:** **Partial.** Borrow the directness of copy ambition. Avoid the "future of" framing, and avoid Doma's mistake of leading with mission abstraction when homebuyers and agents want concrete reassurance about *their* closing.

---

## Cross-cutting observations

**Category baseline (avoid):** Generic blue palette, stock handshake/keys photography, "trusted since YYYY" headlines, dense multi-audience nav, no typographic point of view, paraphrased "open doors / open possibilities" copy.

**Category ceiling (instructive):** Qualia shows what looks modern here — but it's a SaaS ceiling, not a service-brand ceiling. There is *no* title agency website doing distinctive editorial or premium-service design. That gap is Cloud Title's opening.

**Florida-regional check:** Sunbelt and Florida Agency Network reinforce the baseline — they read as 2014 WordPress themes. The regional bar is genuinely low.

**Implication for our three concepts:** Editorial and Premium directions have almost no category competition visually. Data-forward should beat Qualia's discipline without copying the SaaS chassis — closer to a service brand with proof, not a platform with metrics.
