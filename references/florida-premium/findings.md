# Florida / Gulf-Coast Premium — Reference Research

Three sites picked to triangulate Cloud Title's regional register: a Florida luxury brokerage (waterfront aesthetic), a Miami-HQ'd global law firm (B2B authority), and a century-old Florida resort (heritage luxury). Together they bracket what "premium Florida" looks like without sliding into tourist-brochure territory.

---

## 1. ONE Sotheby's International Realty

- **URL:** https://www.onesothebysrealty.com
- **Positioning:** Florida luxury residential brokerage, Miami-anchored, waterfront-focused.
- **Visual treatment:** Dark navy / near-black backgrounds with white serif display headlines paired with a clean sans for nav and body. A muted seawater blue appears in the hero, not as a "beach" cue but as a tonal accent. Full-bleed photography of architecture and water — never sand-and-cocktails. Generous vertical rhythm; sections breathe. Motion is restrained: slow image fades, no parallax theatrics. "Florida" comes through *neighborhood naming* (Coral Gables, Brickell, Coconut Grove) and waterfront geometry — not flora.
- **What works:**
  1. Serif headline + sans body pairing reads as editorial, not realtor-templated.
  2. Dark hero treats the property like a luxury good, not a listing.
  3. Place-naming as identity — local without parochial.
- **Cliché traps to avoid:**
  1. Palm-tree silhouettes against sunset gradients.
  2. Aerial drone shots of South Beach with neon — instant timeshare energy.
  3. Cursive script "signature" fonts pretending to be elegant.
- **Adopt: Partial.** Steal the dark + serif + waterfront-geometry register and the neighborhood-naming move (Cloud Title can name Florida counties / markets the same way). Skip the aspirational lifestyle photography — Cloud Title is B2B-adjacent, not consumer.

## 2. Greenberg Traurig

- **URL:** https://www.gtlaw.com
- **Positioning:** Global law firm, Miami-founded, projecting institutional authority over regional flavor.
- **Visual treatment:** Black-on-white with disciplined whitespace. Modern geometric sans throughout — no serif crutch. Hero is a three-up architectural photo strip ("Adapt. Act. Advance.") with a measured video element. Density is moderate: stats blocks ("51 locations," "860+ Chambers rankings") do the heavy lifting instead of decorative graphics. Motion is functional — no scroll-jacking. No Florida iconography at all; the firm wears its Miami origin invisibly.
- **What works:**
  1. Stats-as-design — credibility expressed numerically, not adjectivally. Direct parallel for Cloud Title's closing volume / on-time record.
  2. Architecture photography (buildings, structure) reads premium without one beach pixel.
  3. Restraint. The page trusts the reader. No "trusted partner" stock photography.
- **Cliché traps to avoid:**
  1. Corporate handshake / diverse-team-around-laptop stock.
  2. Generic blue gradient "professional" header (every title company already does this).
  3. Globe icons and "global reach" graphics for a Florida-only operation.
- **Adopt: Yes.** Closest analog to Cloud Title's B2B authority lane. Adopt the stats-block pattern, architecture-over-people imagery, and discipline to leave whitespace alone.

## 3. The Boca Raton

- **URL:** https://www.thebocaraton.com
- **Positioning:** Century-old Forbes Five-Star Florida resort selling heritage, not sunshine.
- **Visual treatment:** Editorial luxury — typographic hierarchy carries the page, not chrome or gradients. Long-form display headlines ("Surrounded by storied history and grand architecture"). Modular card sections (Beach Club, Yacht Club, Cloister, Spa Palmera) with quiet partitioning. Heritage cues ("Since 1926," "100 years") used as design elements, not just copy. Imagery favors interior architecture and craft detail over generic resort shots.
- **What works:**
  1. Time-depth as luxury signal — dates and history function as design assets.
  2. Named sub-brands (Cloister, Flybridge) create texture; Cloud Title can do this with named service tiers.
  3. Forbes-rating treatment — third-party validation given typographic weight, not a busy badge wall.
- **Cliché traps to avoid:**
  1. Sunset-over-water hero video on loop — every Florida resort does it; instant generic.
  2. Cursive "Welcome to paradise" script overlays.
  3. Gold-on-navy "luxury" combo applied without restraint — turns Ritz-Carlton fast.
- **Adopt: Partial.** Borrow the heritage-as-design-element move (Cloud Title's founding year, closings-to-date, years-in-Florida belong in the type system, not a footer stat). Skip the hospitality warmth — Cloud Title is transactional trust, not leisure.

## Regional flavor playbook — rules for Cloud Title

1. **Florida = water geometry and architecture, never flora.** Coastlines, causeways, mid-century Miami modernism, Sarasota School lines. No palms, no hibiscus, no flamingos — not even ironically.
2. **Light, not heat.** Florida light is the actual differentiator: high-key, hard-edged, slightly bleached. Lean into bright whites and cool blues; avoid orange/coral "warmth" palettes that read tourist-board.
3. **Name the place.** "Closings across Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach, Collier" beats "serving all of Florida." Specificity is the regional cue.
4. **Heritage as type, not ornament.** "Since [year]" set in the display face, sized like a section header — not stuck in a footer badge.
5. **Stats over adjectives.** "94% on-time close rate" carries more Florida-pro authority than "trusted Florida partner."
6. **Banned outright:** sunset gradients, palm silhouettes, script "elegant" fonts, drone Miami-skyline B-roll, gold-on-navy without a reason, generic handshake/laptop stock.
7. **Color discipline:** one cool anchor (deep navy or charcoal), one Florida-light neutral (warm white or bone, never beige), one restrained accent pulled from cloudtitle.com — never invented.
8. **If in doubt, do less.** Greenberg Traurig proves a Miami-born brand can project Florida authority by showing almost no Florida at all.
